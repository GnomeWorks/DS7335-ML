Introduction¶

Original Dataset website: https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/HIGGS

Link to original paper: https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms5308

The data we are looking at is physics data that is used for exotic particle discovery, in particular, the Higgs Boson. By using a deep learning based solution over "shallow" or "traditional" machine larning tools, scientist were able improve classificiation by 8% over standard Machine learning applications.

We will atempt to perform admirably in replicating the studies original findings by testing and evaluating various neural network designs.

Methods¶

Throughout this work, we progressed through the various modifications to the neural net - using different activation functions, different optimization methods, modifying batch size - and observed the results. At the very end, we combined the knowledge gained from our experimentation with prior knowledge of neural networks in an attempt to maximize our AUROC score.

While the walkthrough sections for modifying each of the hyperparameters followed the pattern given ("use three different activation functions," for instance, led us to look up and use three alternate activation functions), our approach with the final model was - effectively - somewhat random. We took the model that had performed the best to date, combined that with some general conclusions drawn from our experiments as a whole (in the form of "X appeared to work better than Y, so let's adjust hyperparameters in the direction of X"), and watched what happened. This was much more of an empirical process of experimentation, and while we were looking to maximize our model's results, our main goal was to glean a better understanding of neural networks in general.

Results¶

Q1: What was the effect of adding more layers/neurons.

Tuning a neural network is sufficiently complex that modifying one hyperparameter - number of layers, or number of neurons - can't really be discussed in a vacuum.

In our early explorations, we experimented with adding layers, more neurons, and more layers with more neurons. The results of these explorations were mixed: later, after working with a "base" model with one hidden layer, we experimented with adding additional layers again, but in the context of altering other hyperparameters (optimization methods, activation functions, learning rate, and so on), which yielded better results as we progressed and further fine-tuned the model.

At this point in the field, it seems that tuning a model is a combination of science, art, and intuition. While our experiments led down what we feel is a successful path with promising results, it is entirely possible that a different approach - layers with a mix of activation functions; differently-tweaked hyperparameters; some means of increasing the number of inputs - could have yielded the same, worse, or better results, and at this time our general understanding of how neural networks is such that each path could be justified.

Understanding the underlying math of a neural network seems to give an edge in adjusting hyperparameters, but it is not (yet) a hard science.

Q2: Which parameters gave you the best result and why (in your opinion) did they work.

Adjusting the batch size and changing the number of epochs seemed to have significant impact on the results of the model, which is sensible: batch size and epoch are both related to how many times the data is run through the model, and so the more time spent training, the more accurate. There were some instances where brute forcing large epoch's did not provide improvement, hence there can be cases of diminishing returns or potential for a level of overfitting.

Of course, there is also the potential for overfitting, which we attempted to account for by adjusting the dropout rate as we adjusted the other hyperparameters. Given that our model has a relatively small amount of total layers (primarily limited by development time and hardware limitations).

Optimizers appeared to have an interesting shift in performance. We did see some advantage come from the adam optimizer in how quickly it was able to hit high performance, but interestingly Stochastic gradient decent provided the best results for our neural networks. This is likely an area where using certain optimizers might be more practical for some applications when one is trying to obtain good results quickly over maximum possible results.

Learning rate also seemed to play a role, albeit a smaller one.

Q3: For #6, how did you decide that your model was done

Humans are terrible at probability prediction, which is why adoption of Bayesian reasoning is so rare in the general population (in that it "feels" wrong to people).

That said, we generally have a "feeling" about given percentages: a 50/50 chance might make us a bit squeamish, while an 80% success rate feels pretty good. People generally seem to act like anything with a probability of around ~.85 is a fairly sure thing, and once you hit ~.95 people seem genuinely surprised when it goes the other way (for a good example of this, see tabletop roleplaying games that use a 20-sided die: while there is always a 5% chance that a given die roll results in a '20,' this result is often treated as either extraordinarily good or horrifically bad, despite the fact that it shows up statistically one in twenty rolls).

For machine learning, this is a bit of a trickier situation. For instance, this writer is aware of a woman with a doctorate in meteorology, who analyzes the coming weather patterns for the benefit of her friends and family. One year, she predicted a nasty tornado in the area, and asked her parents to evacuate, which they did: but the forecasted tornado never came. Her family now no longer trusts her judgments, despite her being right a significant portion of the time otherwise: that one case in which she was wrong has altered their perception of the results she generates.

Machine learning models most likely fall into the same category: people can grasp the idea that a machine can predict things like the weather or their shopping patterns, but due to it being "a computer" may assume that it is infalliable and, when it fails to predict accurately, may assume that the machine is wrong in the future when it is not. These sorts of cognitive biases are the result, again, of humans being terrible prediction engines: rather than understand that the machine is not perfectly accurate, we tend to attribute its failing to something more systemic or indemic to the machine itself, rather than just the foibles of statistics.

This is all to say that there presently don't seem to be any hard and fast rules regarding when a model is done. In a business context there may be concerns about deadlines and resource expenditure, but it may also depend upon the executional context, as well: a model for identifying cancer needs to be significantly more accurate than a model that estimates stock price movement. In general, for anything that doesn't directly risk harm to human life, I would think that something in the ~85% accuracy range would be sufficient, with 90% or higher preferred and something like 75% being an absolute minimum. Given the limitations of our hardware and timelines, we felt we reached a reasonable level of performance and the point of greatly diminishing returns and decided this as a resonable place to pause development.

Conclusion¶

The AUROC for our best model was .832, which seems pretty solid given that we are working with off-the-shelf personal computers and had little prior experience with hyperparameter optimization.

The general feeling that such finetuning of machine learning models is equal parts science, art, and intuition seems to hold out: we expected the final model (AUROC .831) to outperform an earlier one, if only somewhat, but instead it underperformed in comparison. It would seem that even with an understanding of the underlying math and statistical processes at work within a neural network, the actual results are highly contextually-dependent: we feel it would not be safe to assume that any of the things we've gleaned from this work hold in a general sense across the board for all machine learning models.

It seems probable that as an individual working with machine learning on a regular basis has exposure to more and more data sets and more and more models, that a gradual intuition may come to pass: perhaps one activation function behaves better in one context than another, or with a given size of data set, a particular range of batch sizes works best.

A good analogy for this might be lockpicking: your picks will never perfectly fit the lock. While you need to have a solid understanding of the basics to get started, each lock - data problem - has its own foibles and characteristics that make it a unique problem, and each of your lockpicks - hyperparameter tuning - has its uses, with some being more useful in some situations over others.
